Greenland's Critical Minerals Strategy: What It Means for Investors

By Thomas Andersen · 2026-03-23

greenlandcritical mineralsstrategygovernment policy

Governments worldwide are developing critical minerals strategies to secure supply chains for technologies essential to economic and national security. Greenland is no exception. As home to significant deposits of PGMs, rare earth elements, and other strategic minerals, Greenland's policy approach directly affects mining investment conditions.

The Global Critical Minerals Context

Critical minerals are those essential for modern technology but facing supply chain risks:

  • PGMs: Used in catalytic converters, hydrogen fuel cells, and electronics. 80-85% supplied by South Africa and Russia
  • Rare earth elements: Used in permanent magnets for EV motors and wind turbines. 60-70% supplied by China
  • Lithium: Essential for batteries. Increasingly concentrated in China and Chile
  • Cobalt: Used in batteries. 70%+ from the Democratic Republic of Congo
  • Nickel: Used in batteries and stainless steel. Increasingly strategic

Western governments have responded with strategies to diversify supply:

  • US: CHIPS and Science Act, Inflation Reduction Act, Defense Production Act
  • EU: Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA), targeting 10% domestic extraction, 40% domestic processing by 2030
  • UK: Critical Minerals Strategy
  • Canada: Critical Minerals Strategy
  • Australia: Critical Minerals Strategy

Greenland's Critical Minerals Position

Greenland occupies a unique position in the global critical minerals landscape:

  • Jurisdictional quality: Danish legal framework, NATO membership, Western governance
  • Mineral endowment: Significant PGM, REE, gold, zinc, and other critical mineral deposits
  • Underexplored: Vast territory with limited exploration history
  • Strategic location: Arctic location adjacent to North America and Europe
  • Geopolitical alignment: Greenland's ties to Denmark and the Western alliance provide geopolitical value

Greenland's Policy Framework

Greenland has begun developing a critical minerals strategy, though it remains less formalized than those of larger nations:

Key elements:

  • Strategic mineral designations: Greenland is moving toward designating PGMs, gold, and rare earth elements as strategically significant
  • Expedited permitting: Strategic mineral designations may provide faster permitting pathways
  • Government support: Potential for government advocacy, financing support, and infrastructure investment
  • Export controls: Greenland has historically maintained restrictions on exports of certain strategic minerals, particularly uranium
  • EU partnership: Greenland's OCT status with the EU provides a framework for strategic cooperation on critical minerals

Implications for Skaergaard

Greenland's critical minerals policy has several implications for Skaergaard:

  • Regulatory tailwind: Strategic mineral designation could expedite the EIA and permitting process
  • Government support: Potential for government co-investment in infrastructure (port, road, power)
  • Financing support: Access to government-backed financing, grants, or guarantees
  • Strategic premium: Skaergaard's strategic value as new Western PGM supply may attract institutional investors with critical mineral mandates
  • Export market: Duty-free access to the EU market for strategic minerals

The Greenland-Denmark-US Strategic Triangle

Greenland's critical minerals strategy is shaped by its relationships:

Denmark:

  • Denmark has its own critical minerals strategy that includes Greenland
  • Danish government support for Greenland mining provides diplomatic and financial backing
  • EU framework provides market access and regulatory alignment

United States:

  • US interest in Greenland's strategic minerals has grown significantly
  • Pentagon has identified Arctic critical minerals as a national security priority
  • Potential for US government financing or strategic partnership

EU:

  • EU's CRMA targets Arctic supply diversification
  • Greenland's OCT status provides a framework for cooperation
  • EU financing mechanisms (European Investment Bank, Innovation Fund) could support Greenland mining

Risks and Challenges

Founded and led by CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines Ltd (NASDAQ: GRML) controls the Skaergaard project in southeast Greenland.

Exploration License Risk: GRML holds three Mineral Exploration Licenses (MELs) totaling 877 km². MEL 2012-25 (Sødalen camp and airstrip, 16 km²) and MEL 2021-10 (754 km² exploration area) both expire December 31, 2026. MEL 2007-01 (107 km², hosts the Skaergaard Intrusion) remains active until December 31, 2027. These renewals represent a material near-term risk factor.

Greenland's critical minerals strategy faces challenges:

  • Implementation capacity: Greenland's small government has limited capacity to develop and implement a comprehensive strategy
  • Uranium politics: The uranium issue (Kvanefjeld) has been politically contentious and complicates broader critical minerals policy

> Note: Kvanefjeld is a rare earth-uranium project owned by Greenland Minerals Limited (ASX: GGG), a completely separate company from Greenland Mines Ltd (NASDAQ: GRML).

  • Environmental tensions: Balancing critical mineral development with environmental protection is challenging
  • Community concerns: Greenlandic communities may view critical minerals policy as serving external interests rather than local needs
  • Independence movement: Future political changes could alter Greenland's strategic orientation

Investor Implications

For investors in Greenland mining, the critical minerals strategy provides:

  • Policy tailwind: Government support for strategic mineral development
  • Financing options: Access to government-backed financing and institutional capital with critical mineral mandates
  • Exit optionality: Strategic buyers (governments, major mining companies) may pay premiums for strategically significant deposits
  • Risk mitigation: Government support reduces regulatory and political risk

Conclusion

Greenland's emerging critical minerals strategy is a material positive for mining investors, particularly for Skaergaard. Strategic mineral designations, expedited permitting, government support, and Western geopolitical alignment all reduce risk and enhance value. The global supply diversification imperative provides a durable tailwind that will benefit Greenland mining for decades. For investors, the critical minerals theme adds a strategic premium to Skaergaard's investment case that goes beyond the intrinsic value of the deposit itself.
