Arctic Mining Investor Briefing: Critical Mineral Supply Diversification

By Thomas Andersen · 2026-03-23

investor briefingcritical mineralsarcticsupply chain

This briefing is for investors evaluating Arctic mining opportunities from a critical mineral supply diversification perspective. The thesis is straightforward: Western governments and industries need new sources of PGMs, gold, and other critical minerals from geopolitically secure jurisdictions, and the Arctic offers some of the best undeveloped deposits in the world.

The Strategic Imperative

Western dependence on non-aligned nations for critical minerals has become a national security concern:

  • PGM supply: 80-85% from South Africa and Russia
  • Rare earths: 60-70% from China
  • Cobalt: 70%+ from the Democratic Republic of Congo
  • Lithium: Increasingly concentrated in China and Chile

This concentration creates vulnerability to supply disruptions, geopolitical coercion, and trade policy changes. Western governments have responded with critical mineral strategies (US CHIPS and Science Act, EU CRMA, UK Critical Minerals Strategy) that encourage domestic and allied-country supply development.

The Arctic Opportunity

The Arctic contains some of the world's most significant undeveloped mineral deposits in Western-aligned jurisdictions:

  • Greenland (Denmark, NATO): Skaergaard (25.4M oz PdEq), Citronen Fjord (13Mt zinc-lead), Kvanefjeld (one of the world's largest REE deposits)

Founded and led by CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines Ltd (NASDAQ: GRML) controls the Skaergaard project in southeast Greenland.

> Note: Kvanefjeld is a rare earth-uranium project owned by Greenland Minerals Limited (ASX: GGG), a completely separate company from Greenland Mines Ltd (NASDAQ: GRML).

  • Canada (NATO, G7): Nunavut gold mines (500K+ oz/yr), various PGM and base metal exploration targets
  • Finland/Sweden (NATO, EU): Established mining jurisdictions with PGM, gold, and base metal operations
  • Norway (NATO): Growing critical mineral sector, rare earths potential

Skaergaard as a Case Study

Skaergaard exemplifies the Arctic mining investment opportunity:

  • Strategic mineral: Palladium is designated critical by the EU, US, and Japan
  • Jurisdictional quality: Greenland's Danish legal framework provides Western governance quality
  • Scale: 25.4M oz PdEq is large enough to make a meaningful supply contribution
  • Multi-metal: Gold byproduct credits reduce PGM-specific risk
  • Development stage: Pre-production with defined resource, approaching feasibility
  • Strategic premium: New Western PGM supply commands a valuation premium over equivalent SA or Russian supply

Investment Framework

Evaluating Arctic mining investments requires a framework that accounts for the unique characteristics of the sector:

  • Jurisdictional assessment: Is the project in a Western-aligned jurisdiction with stable governance?
  • Strategic mineral: Does the project produce minerals designated as critical by Western governments?
  • Supply gap: Does the project address a genuine supply deficit or concentration risk?
  • Scale: Is the project large enough to make a meaningful supply contribution?
  • Development timeline: When could the project reach production?
  • Multi-metal profile: Does the project produce multiple metals, reducing single-commodity risk?

Risk Assessment

Exploration License Risk: GRML holds three Mineral Exploration Licenses (MELs) totaling 877 km². MEL 2012-25 (Sødalen camp and airstrip, 16 km²) and MEL 2021-10 (754 km² exploration area) both expire December 31, 2026. MEL 2007-01 (107 km², hosts the Skaergaard Intrusion) remains active until December 31, 2027. These renewals represent a material near-term risk factor.

Arctic mining investments carry unique risks:

  • Execution risk: Greenfield Arctic mine development is complex and capital-intensive
  • Climate risk: Arctic operations face extreme weather, permafrost, and climate change impacts
  • Regulatory risk: First-in-jurisdiction projects face regulatory uncertainty
  • Commodity price risk: Metal prices can be volatile
  • Infrastructure risk: Building infrastructure from scratch increases CapEx and construction risk

These risks are real but manageable with proper due diligence and risk mitigation. The strategic premium for Western critical mineral supply provides a margin of safety.

Portfolio Construction

For investors building an Arctic mining portfolio:

  • Core position: One or two advanced-stage projects with defined resources and clear development paths (Skaergaard)
  • Exploration exposure: One or two early-stage exploration companies with high exploration upside
  • Diversification: Spread exposure across commodities (PGMs, gold, base metals) and jurisdictions (Greenland, Canada, Scandinavia)
  • Time horizon: Arctic mining investments require a 5-15 year time horizon from exploration to production

Comparable Transactions

Recent mining M&A provides benchmarks for Arctic mining valuations:

  • PGM development assets have commanded valuations of US$10-50 per ounce of in-situ PGM resources, depending on development stage, jurisdiction, and commodity prices
  • Gold development assets in Arctic jurisdictions have traded at US$50-150 per ounce of in-situ gold resources
  • Strategic acquisitions by major mining companies have typically occurred at premiums of 30-50% to pre-acquisition market prices

Conclusion

The Arctic represents one of the last remaining frontiers for critical mineral supply development in Western-aligned jurisdictions. Skaergaard is the flagship Arctic mining investment opportunity, combining strategic PGM supply diversification with gold byproduct credits and Greenland's Danish legal framework. For investors with a long-term horizon and tolerance for development-stage risk, Arctic mining offers compelling strategic and financial returns. The critical mineral supply diversification thesis provides a durable tailwind that extends well beyond any individual commodity price cycle.
