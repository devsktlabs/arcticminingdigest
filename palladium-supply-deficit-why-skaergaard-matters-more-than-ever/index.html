Palladium Supply Deficit: Why Skaergaard Matters More Than Ever

By Thomas Andersen · 2026-03-23

palladiumsupply deficitskaergaardPGM market

The palladium market is facing a structural supply deficit that shows no sign of resolving. South African production is declining, Russian supply faces geopolitical constraints, and new mine development has not kept pace. This article examines the supply-demand dynamics and why Skaergaard is strategically positioned.

The Supply Side

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

South Africa (60-65% of primary supply):

  • Bushveld Complex production has been declining for years due to falling grades, deeper mining, energy constraints, and labor challenges
  • Major producers (Sibanye-Stillwater, Implats, Amplats) have reported production declines of 5-15% over the past five years
  • New underground development at Platreef (Ivanhoe) will add some supply, but not enough to offset declines
  • Energy crisis (load shedding) has periodically reduced mining production
  • Capital investment in new SA PGM capacity has been limited due to regulatory, political, and economic challenges

Russia (25-30% of primary supply):

  • Norilsk Nickel remains the world's largest palladium producer
  • Sanctions have disrupted Russian PGM trade, redirecting supply toward Asian markets
  • Access to Russian PGMs is restricted for Western manufacturers, creating supply uncertainty
  • Norilsk has announced production guidance reductions in recent periods

Recycling (20-25% of total supply):

  • Catalytic converter recycling is the primary source of secondary palladium
  • Recycling supply is growing but cannot fill the primary supply gap
  • Collection rates for end-of-life catalytic converters vary by region (higher in developed countries, lower in developing)

New supply projects:

  • Very few new primary PGM projects are in development globally
  • Skaergaard is the most significant new Western-aligned PGM project
  • Development timelines are long (8-15 years from exploration to production)

The Demand Side

Automotive catalytic converters (70-75% of demand):

  • PGM demand from internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles remains the largest demand segment
  • ICE vehicle production continues globally, particularly in developing markets
  • Hybrid vehicles (HEV and PHEV) use more PGMs per vehicle than pure ICE vehicles due to more stringent emissions standards
  • The EV transition is reducing PGM demand from the automotive sector, but the transition is slower than many forecasts predicted

Hydrogen fuel cells (growing):

  • Palladium is used in proton exchange membrane (PEM) fuel cells
  • Hydrogen fuel cell adoption is accelerating, particularly for commercial vehicles and stationary power
  • Fuel cell demand could become a significant new demand source for palladium by 2030-2035

Industrial applications (10-15%):

  • Electronics, chemical processing, dental, and other industrial uses
  • Relatively stable demand, less cyclical than automotive

Investment demand (variable):

  • Palladium ETFs and physical investment demand
  • Can be significant during periods of supply concern or price appreciation

The Deficit

The palladium supply deficit is estimated at:

  • Current deficit: 0.5-1.0 million ounces per year
  • Projected deficit: 1.0-2.0 million ounces per year by 2030, assuming current production trends and demand projections
  • Above-ground stocks: Have been drawn down significantly over the past decade, providing a buffer that is now largely depleted

A structural supply deficit means that demand consistently exceeds supply, requiring above-ground stock draws to balance the market. When above-ground stocks are depleted, prices must rise enough to demand destruction to restore balance.

Why Skaergaard Matters

Skaergaard's potential annual production of 200,000-400,000 oz palladium would represent:

  • 5-10% of new Western supply need: Meaningful contribution to filling the supply gap
  • Diversification: New supply from a non-SA, non-Russian source
  • Strategic value: Western-aligned supply accessible to all markets
  • Multi-metal: Gold and rhodium byproduct credits reduce effective palladium production costs

At full production, Skaergaard could supply approximately 2-4% of total global palladium demand, making it a material source of new supply.

Price Implications

The structural deficit has implications for palladium prices:

  • Price support: The deficit provides a floor under palladium prices. Even in a demand downturn, the supply constraint limits price downside
  • Upside potential: If the deficit deepens as projected, palladium prices could rise significantly above current levels
  • Volatility: Tight markets are more volatile. Palladium price volatility is likely to persist

The Counter-Argument

Some analysts argue the palladium deficit will resolve through:

  • EV adoption: Accelerating EV adoption will reduce automotive PGM demand
  • Platinum substitution: Platinum can substitute for palladium in catalytic converters at the right price ratio
  • New supply: Higher prices will incentivize new supply development

These factors provide some demand-side relief but are unlikely to fully close the deficit within the next decade, given the long lead time for new mine development.

Conclusion

The palladium supply deficit is structural, driven by declining SA production, constrained Russian supply, and insufficient new mine development. Skaergaard is the most significant new Western-aligned PGM project in development and is strategically positioned to contribute new supply. For investors, the deficit provides a durable price-supportive fundamental backdrop that enhances the investment case for Skaergaard.
